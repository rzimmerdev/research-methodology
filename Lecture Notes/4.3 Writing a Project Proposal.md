Before committing yourself and your peers to extensively investigate the research questions you came up with, a highly common and often required document for formal research is the **project proposal**. In this document, you should formalize the topic you're going to tackle. It marks the transition from a scratch project that broadly discusses some ideas to a more structured approach to investigate the question, ensuring it the methods, algorithms and ideas are well-defined and most importantly also significant to the objective of solving the questions.

It is natural to spend some time on writing the proposal itself before doing any research, and this might feel cumbersome and unproductive as you're not actually doing any maths or programming. However, remember that this phase is essential to organize your objectives, both for yourself and for your colleagues and P.I.s. It might even help you to fill up gaps in your question or literature you've gathered up until now. 

Note: we will discuss the relevance of gathering literature and when/how you should actively read papers, as well as how to create a proper bibliography and list of references to eventually use in your write-up/scientific paper.

## The Project Proposal

As mentioned briefly in previous lectures, the core of your proposal is the _**research agenda**_. This serves as the "one true source" where you explain the background and the appeal of your chosen problem, as well as the steps you plan to follow to attack the problem. Your agenda must outline the initial stages of your investigation, acting as a experimental design that identifies the object of your study (in mathematics and CS, as well as other STEM research topics, this is often in terms of **variables you will study**) and the **data you will collect**. This formal plan provides the initial sense of direction needed to move from aimless tinkering toward a clear quantitative purpose.

To start with your proposal, we give you a general outline of what it should contain, but note that this is by no means an extensive layout, and more often than not you will have to both adapt it to your own personal taste and project/topic requirements. Additionally, your lab or colleagues you know might have done proposals before, and you could ask them for theirs to use as inspiration as well, both in terms of the outline as well as content.

A high-quality proposal should contain at least some of the following sections, and is built with the clear objective of formally defining the question and the steps you plan to take to solve it:

Note: usually a project proposal is also required for submitting your project to institutions or scholarship award, so sections regarding authors, student researchers, chair of origin and similar might come first.

1. Statement of the Problem and Introduction

Up to this point, you should already have thought of and considered some research questions. Carefully consider each of them using the criteria we discussed in [[4.2 Evaluating the quality of a Question]], and chose the one that best fits your interest, background, timeline constraints, and other external requirements you might have.

Clearly define the specific question or questions you plan to examine. This section "sets the stage" by explaining the source of your inspiration and why the problem is interesting or important to you.

2. Short Literature Review

If you are building upon existing work, you should summarize the current "state of the art" in your research area. Even if you have not identified a "direct ancestor" of your problem, this section is fundamental to show both yourself and your group that you have taken the necessary precautions to analyze both the novelty and feasibility of the project by looking at existing approaches. 
This also involves identifying relevant studies or techniques that must be looked at and mastered up to some point to proceed with the research.

3. Motivation

This section motivates the entire work that will be done. It should reference the literature review you've performed and justify both why the research question is interesting as well as what solving it achieves in your field. You can organize this section as you feel is necessary to demonstrate the point you want to make with your project. But take into consideration that the main purpose of this section is to motivate the reader into understanding the relevance of posing and solving the research question is. They should have a sense of why it is an important question and what solving it will achieve, so aim to inspire them enough to back you up and understand the project is important.

4. Background, Objective and Contributions

Again, this section should also reference the literature review, so make sure the review has been done seriously as it is essential to the proposal. Here you should shortly familiarize the reader with the necessary background to understand the terminology, the algorithms and the mathematical tools one needs to reproduce your research. 


Additionally, this section should contain your "Objective". This is an extremely essential element in both the proposal and your project. You should have clearly in your mind what does it mean to answer your question. 
Do you plan to answer it by proving a theorem, gathering enough data to statistically justify an assumption, or something else? It formalizes your question and gives the reader a clear expected result to look out for, so they understand what your contributions should be beforehand. This should also guide you into planning your experiments and conjectures you will try to prove or discuss, and you could separate this section into a "Objectives and Contributions" section if you feel it ends up more organized.


5. Research Agenda and Plan

This section should both summarize your proposal up until this point, as well as lay out the steps you plan to take during the duration of the project. It helps to have a fixed timestamp for each of the steps, as well as an order in which you plan to take them. If you have a deadline for the project, even better, as you can carefully plan everything accordingly.

## Evaluation and Peer-Review

A good idea for writing your proposal is to go over it with your P.I.s and your colleagues. If you are doing the project by yourself or a smaller independent group, you can even go over it with your family or friends (also a good idea to see how well you described the ideas and definitions, as these will be people not familiar with the topic in hand).
The review should also evaluate if the focus of the project is too broad or too specific, and if the question is both **significant** and **clear** enough, so the peer review step is very important altogether. 

You should not think of peer-reviewing as optional, even though you could always have been lucky (as we mentioned, even the most proficient researchers have a hard time coming up with interesting research questions). The peer-review process ensures the project clearly summarized what you want to do and that the agenda also provides an achievable/realistic goal for the planned time interval and context. 

A well-prepared proposal ultimately leads to a more satisfying research experience and a more rigorous final report.

It is a helpful tool to keep in mind when working on your research, and can more often than not help you get unstuck in a hard programming or thinking session. Be sure to pay attention to it and remembering the overall idea of the cycle that is making sure you always make progress, even if you have to go back sometimes.
