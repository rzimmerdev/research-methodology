Choosing a research topic involves moving from aimless variations or ideas toward a clear problem statement and planned objectives, where questions build upon one another aiming to create a cohesive body of work, i.e., with beginning, middle, and end.

Research topics rarely emerge from a vacuum. They are often inspired by existing contexts or settings that direct your line of thought.

- Research Settings: These are mathematical situations or objects that do not come with an initial problem statement. These are usually harder for new researchers, as they require more maturity in using available literature, as well as usually requiring long bibliographical reviews to lay out existing approaches.
- Existing Problems: You can find inspiration in mundane textbook questions, strategy games, and even simple well-known puzzles. A great example that became quite popular recently are the Erdos Problems, but similar compendiums also exist, like the Polymath Project. One might even find interesting research questions in Kaggle or even on theoretical pages of Stack Overflow. By identifying the explicit and implicit conditions of these problems, you can begin to alter them to create original research agendas, which is in itself a technique used by professional researchers.
- "Low Threshold, High Ceiling" Projects: For those new to research, it is often best to choose topics with a "low threshold" that requires minimal background to start, but a "high (enough) ceiling," offering deep and sophisticated challenges for exploration without feeling too cumbersome or impossible to be demotivating.


Once you have a starting point, you can use known techniques for posing problems to generate or expand the specific research topic by applying these techniques.

1. **Change the Numbers:** Identify every stated and implied number in a problem (such as dimensions, angles, or the number of objects) and experiment with different values.
2. **Change the Geometry:** Alter the shapes involved, move from two dimensions to three, or change the structure of the space (e.g., moving a game from a square grid to a triangular tiling).
3. **Change the Operation:** Switch between different algebraic operations, geometric transformations (like rotating instead of scaling), or substitute predictable behaviors for random ones.
4. **Remove or Add Conditions:** Loosen a definition to create a more general case, or add new constraints to see how they restrict the original problem.
5. **Repeat a Process (Iteration):** Explore what happens when you repeatedly apply a function or operation, which can lead to complex and beautiful mathematical structures.
6. **Change the Objective:** Shift the focus of the inquiry. Instead of finding a single solution, ask "How many solutions exist?", "What is the maximum or minimum value?", or "Can I find an algorithm to list every possibility?".

> Note these are very mathematical approaches to posing problems, and you might have to adapt these if you plan to expand an existing question in another field. Do note that the approach remains fairly similar, in which the existing question is changed in terms of some operation, condition, initial assumptions or final expected observations for example.

Additionally, to ensure a topic is adequate for your context, you must make sure the topic checks at least the following items, so you will both be able to finish the project in a reasonable amount of time, with an interesting enough result and without feeling demotivated or giving up.

1. **Intrinsic Interest:** You should have a personal interest in the problem; your own **mathematical aesthetic** will drive your persistence and this is by far the most important item in this list.
2. **Background Knowledge:** Ensure you possess, or can realistically acquire, the technical skills necessary to make headway. This requirement is important so you don't feel overwhelmed and end up constantly stuck spending more time learning the required baggage than actually doing research.
3. **Challenge vs. Feasibility:** A good topic should be challenging enough that there is no obvious method of attack, but it should not be a famous, unsolved problem that has stumped professionals for decades.
4. **Originality:** The most exciting projects involve **uncharted territory** where no solution is already lurking in a textbook, and are also not too specific in application. 
5. **Practical Constraints:** The topic must be realistic for the time allotted and the resources (such as specific technology or mentors) available to you.


---

Before committing extensive time to research, you should formalize your topic in a **project proposal**. We will see this briefly in the next lecture on "Finding a Research Question", and more in-depth in "Writing a Project Proposal", where you will take your research question and formalize it. It is natural to have many iterations of a research question, and even end up with more than one question. 

The project document should clearly state your ***research agenda***, remember this term, as it will be the one true source where you will explain the background and the appeal of the problem. It should outline the initial steps you plan to take, such as the data you will collect and the variables you will study and will give you an initial sense of direction on how to proceed.
