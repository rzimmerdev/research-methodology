Research methodology is a meta-topic in research, and consists of methods and theory related to how one should perform research, going from how to find research topics and performing experiments to performing literature review and structuring research papers.

Methodology is not an optional choice when doing research, and one will always do some form of methodology when running experiments or writing a paper, even if they do not know they are following some methodology themselves.
Thus, it is very important for the journey of becoming a proficient researcher to know and understand what the term methodology itself means and how it influences how research is done. There are entire fields dedicated to studying the different techniques and forms of doing methodology, but in this lecture series we will focus on simple approaches and terminology, serving more as an introduction than a complete in-depth review of all experimental and descriptive methods.

We will start mentioning some of the more common methodology techniques used and discussed in the field.
When one talks about methodology in research, the usual understanding is the different research methods that exist.
Some common categories in different fields of knowledge are:
- Philosophical Research: focuses on the vision and definitions of knowledge, usually how these relate between different points of views, socially or academically.
- Historical Research: discusses past events that happened in time, and can be both qualitative or quantitative in the descriptions and results obtained. 
- Survey Research: deals with current events happening at present time, and even though questions posed to subjects can be qualitative, the research performed is quantitative. Sub-divided into: discretionary, correlational and exploratory type of research. 
- Experimental Research: quantitative in nature, and deals with making assumptions and experiments to validate such assumptions, with the objective of demonstrating some persistent rule.
- Case-Study Research: the research method focused on a single observation of a specific event.

For example, researchers interested in marketing and in coming up with new campaigns are usually interested in survey research, and have come up with a series of steps in performing validated surveys.

For mathematics and computer science, as well as for most STEM fields, the usual research method is the [scientific method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_method), and although meta research in STEM fields is not that common, there is still a lot of theory and baggage one could learn about the scientific method and its extensions. In summary, as you might already know from school, the scientific method consists succinctly of observing phenomena, posing a statement or assumption about said phenomena, and performing logically related experiments with the objective of validating the assumptions made.

We could end the lecture series here, as the scientific method is in itself enough to perform valid research in STEM fields. However, a lot has been discussed since Aristotle and also the [Edwin Smith papyrus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwin_Smith_papyrus "Edwin Smith papyrus"), and improvements in terms of how to actually perform the individual steps are of great interest to the modern scientist. While we will not go that much in depth over extensions of the scientific method, the lecture series links to some very interesting bibliography related to this topic.
