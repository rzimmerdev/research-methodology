1. **Setting / Topic Selection**  
    Begin with a general area of interest. This could be a structure, pattern, or domain (e.g., graphs, sequences, geometric configurations). We look at this in [[3. Choosing a Topic]]
    
2. **Formulate a Question**  
    Turn your setting into a concrete, investigable question. At this stage, the question may still be informal or exploratory. We look at this in [[4.1 Finding a Research Question]] and [[4.2 Evaluating the quality of a Question]]
    
3. **Understand and Define the Question**  
    Clarify all terms and conditions:
    
    - Define objects precisely
    - Identify constraints and assumptions
    - Restate the question in a rigorous form
    
    This step often reveals hidden ambiguities or suggests refinements. We look at this in more detail in [[4.3 Writing a Project Proposal]]
    
4. **Explore Through Observation**  
    Investigate examples:
    
    - Compute small cases
    - Draw diagrams or run simulations
    - Look for patterns, invariants, or anomalies
    
    This step is often very specific to your topic and field of interest, and you should ensure your work is valid and rigorous enough. We suggest you look to other existing references to have a general sense of how your own work should look like.

    
5. **Form Conjectures and Assumptions**  
    Based on observations, propose tentative claims:
    
    - What do you think is always true?
    - Under what conditions might it fail?
    
6. **Build Connections**  
    Relate your conjecture to known ideas
    - Similar problems or theorems
    - Analogies in other areas
    - Useful tools or frameworks

The following steps will tell you if you have successfully concluded the project, or should go back and revisit your previous idea. This is a natural step in research, and the more you do it the better you will be at tracking your work back and reformulating your ideas. Do not feel bad with yourself if you constantly have to go back, as it is natural to reevaluate your work and even the best of the best do it constantly.

    
7. **Test the Conjecture**  
    Attempt to:
    
    - **Find counterexamples** (to disprove), or
    - **Construct a proof** (to confirm)
    
    Both directions are equally valuable and often inform each other. You might more often than not find published only works that actually end up with successful proofs, but rigorous science includes both proving or disproving claims equally.
    
8. **Iterate and Refine**  
    If the conjecture fails or the proof stalls. This step is exactly what makes up the research cycle, and as you get better you will not fail less, but rather just learn to fail faster and continue your work without being stuck for too long on wrong directions. When you feel you've hit a dead end, or your results do not validate your claims you should
    
    - Modify the statement
    - Adjust assumptions
    - Return to earlier steps with new insight
